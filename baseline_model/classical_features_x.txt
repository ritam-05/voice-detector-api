CLASSICAL AUDIO FEATURES USED (16 TOTAL)

The following 16 handcrafted audio features were used in the classical
machine learning model (Random Forest / LightGBM / XGBoost).

These features summarize low-level acoustic properties of speech and
were extracted per audio segment.


1–13. MFCC (Mel-Frequency Cepstral Coefficients)


MFCCs represent the short-term spectral envelope of speech
in a way that approximates human auditory perception.

Each MFCC captures information about:
- vocal tract shape
- spectral energy distribution
- phonetic structure

In this project:
- 13 MFCC coefficients were computed per audio segment
- The mean value of each coefficient over time was used

Limitation:
MFCCs capture spectral shape but lose fine temporal and phase information,
making them insufficient to distinguish clean human speech from TTS.


14. Spectral Centroid


Represents the "center of mass" of the audio spectrum.

High centroid:
- brighter, sharper sounds

Low centroid:
- darker, muffled sounds

Used to capture overall timbre characteristics.

Limitation:
Studio-recorded human speech and AI-generated speech often have
very similar spectral centroids.


15. Spectral Bandwidth


Measures how spread out the frequencies are around the centroid.

High bandwidth:
- noisy or complex signals

Low bandwidth:
- smooth, clean signals

Limitation:
Clean human recordings and TTS both exhibit low bandwidth,
making them difficult to distinguish.


16. Zero Crossing Rate (ZCR)


Counts how often the audio signal crosses zero amplitude.

High ZCR:
- noisy or unvoiced sounds

Low ZCR:
- smooth, voiced speech

Limitation:
TTS and clean human speech both have low ZCR,
leading to ambiguity.


OVERALL LIMITATION OF THESE FEATURES


These 16 features describe how the audio was recorded,
not how speech was physiologically produced.

As a result:
- Studio-quality human speech can be acoustically indistinguishable from TTS
- The model relies on recording artifacts rather than speech production cues

This limitation motivated the transition to deep learning
using self-supervised speech models (e.g., wav2vec2),
which learn speech production characteristics directly from raw audio.
